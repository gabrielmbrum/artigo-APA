\section{Theoretical Foundation}
\label{sec:theory}

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Fractal Analysis and Dimension Estimation}
\label{sec:theory:fractal}

Fractal geometry provides a mathematical framework for
characterising structures whose complexity persists across
observation scales, a property termed \textit{statistical
self-similarity}~\cite{Mandelbrot1982}. Fractal sets exhibit
power-law scaling: a characteristic measure $M(\varepsilon)$
obtained at scale $\varepsilon$ satisfies

\begin{equation}
  M(\varepsilon) \;\sim\; C \cdot \varepsilon^{-D},
  \label{eq:powerlaw}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $C$ is a proportionality constant and $D$ is the
fractal dimension~\cite{Falconer2003}. Taking logarithms of both
sides yields the linear relationship

\begin{equation}
  \log M(\varepsilon) = \log C - D \log \varepsilon,
  \label{eq:loglog}
\end{equation}

\noindent from which $D$ is recovered as the negative slope of a
least-squares regression over the log-scale pairs
$\{(\log \varepsilon_i, \log M(\varepsilon_i))\}_{i=1}^{m}$,
computed across $m$ discrete scales.

Because the Hausdorff dimension~\cite{Hausdorff1919} requires an
infimum over all possible coverings, it is not directly computable
on discrete pixel grids. The Minkowski--Bouligand (box-counting)
dimension $D_B$ provides a tractable approximation~\cite{Falconer2003}:

\begin{equation}
  D_B = \lim_{\varepsilon \to 0}
        \frac{\log N(\varepsilon)}{\log(1/\varepsilon)},
  \label{eq:boxdim}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $N(\varepsilon)$ is the minimum number of boxes of
side $\varepsilon$ needed to cover the set. In practice,
Eq.~\eqref{eq:boxdim} is approximated by the finite regression of
Eq.~\eqref{eq:loglog} over a scale set
$\mathcal{S} = \{\varepsilon_1 > \varepsilon_2 > \cdots > \varepsilon_m\}$.
The quality of the estimate is assessed through the coefficient of
determination $R^{2}$ of the regression: values close to unity
confirm consistent power-law scaling and are a necessary condition
for interpreting $D_B$ as a meaningful fractal
descriptor~\cite{Lopes2011}.

\subsection{Classical Box-Counting Method}
\label{sec:theory:bc}

The Classical Box-Counting~(BC) method is the direct
discretisation of Eq.~\eqref{eq:boxdim} for binary
images~\cite{Mandelbrot1982,Liebovitch1989}. Let $I$ be a binary
image of $N = W \times H$ pixels. For a given box side
$\varepsilon$, the image is partitioned into
$\lceil W/\varepsilon \rceil \times \lceil H/\varepsilon \rceil$
non-overlapping cells; a cell is \textit{occupied} if it contains
at least one foreground pixel:

\begin{equation}
  N(\varepsilon)
    = \sum_{i=0}^{\lceil W/\varepsilon \rceil - 1}
      \sum_{j=0}^{\lceil H/\varepsilon \rceil - 1}
      \mathbf{1}\!\left[
        \exists\,(x,y) \in B_{ij}(\varepsilon) : I(x,y) = 1
      \right].
  \label{eq:bc_count}
\end{equation}

Because BC operates on binary images, a prior binarisation step is
required when the input is a grayscale medical image. The standard
strategy is Otsu's global thresholding~\cite{Otsu1979}, which
selects the threshold $t^{*}$ that minimises intra-class intensity
variance. This step introduces an inherent trade-off: binarisation
lowers the computational cost of the subsequent coverage test, but
irreversibly discards intensity gradient information, making the
estimated $D_B$ sensitive to the choice of $t^{*}$.

To avoid scanning all $\varepsilon^{2}$ pixels per cell, the
optimised formulation pre-computes the \textit{integral image}
$P[x][y] = \sum_{i \leq x, j \leq y} I[i][j]$ using the
recurrence~\cite{Crow1984}

\begin{equation}
  P[x][y]
    = I[x][y] + P[x{-}1][y] + P[x][y{-}1] - P[x{-}1][y{-}1].
  \label{eq:prefix}
\end{equation}

\noindent Given $P$, the foreground pixel count within any
rectangular cell is retrieved in $\mathcal{O}(1)$ as

\begin{equation}
  \sigma(x_1,y_1,x_2,y_2) =
    \begin{aligned}[t]
      &P[x_2][y_2] - P[x_1{-}1][y_2] \\
      &- P[x_2][y_1{-}1] + P[x_1{-}1][y_1{-}1],
    \end{aligned}
  \label{eq:rect_sum}
\end{equation}

\noindent with occupancy established by $\sigma > 0$. The integral
image is built once in $\Theta(N)$ time and space, and shared
across all scales in $\mathcal{S}$.

\subsection{Gliding Box Method}
\label{sec:theory:gb}

The Gliding Box~(GB) method~\cite{Allain1991} replaces the
non-overlapping partition of BC with a \textit{dense sliding
window}: a box of side $L$ is placed at every valid pixel
position $(x, y)$, and the \textit{box mass}

\begin{equation}
  m_{x,y}(L) = \sum_{i=0}^{L-1} \sum_{j=0}^{L-1} I[x+i][y+j]
  \label{eq:gb_mass}
\end{equation}

\noindent is recorded for each of the $T(L) = (W-L+1)(H-L+1)$
valid placements. The resulting \textit{mass-frequency
distribution} $n(m, L)$ counts how many placements yield mass $m$,
giving the normalised probability $P(m, L) = n(m, L) / T(L)$.

The fractal dimension is estimated from the mean box mass

\begin{equation}
  \mu(L) = \sum_{m=0}^{L^{2}} m \, P(m, L),
  \label{eq:gb_mu1}
\end{equation}

\noindent by substituting $\mu(L)$ for $M(\varepsilon)$ in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:loglog} and fitting the slope over
$\mathcal{S}$~\cite{Plotnick1996}. A complementary descriptor,
\textit{lacunarity}, is obtained from the first two moments of the
distribution:

\begin{equation}
  \Lambda(L)
    = \frac{\mu^{(2)}(L) - [\mu(L)]^{2}}{[\mu(L)]^{2}},
  \label{eq:lacunarity}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\mu^{(2)}(L) = \sum_{m} m^{2} P(m, L)$.
Lacunarity quantifies translational heterogeneity: a homogeneous
set yields $\Lambda \approx 0$, while a clustered or gapped
structure yields large $\Lambda$~\cite{Allain1991,Tolle2008}. The
joint use of $D_B$ and $\Lambda$ has been shown to improve tissue
discriminability in histological image
classification~\cite{Roberto2021,Borgue2025}, since two textures
may share a similar $D_B$ yet differ in their spatial mass
distribution.

The same integral-image structure of Eq.~\eqref{eq:prefix}
applies to GB: pre-computing $P$ reduces each mass evaluation from
$\Theta(L^{2})$ to $\mathcal{O}(1)$ via Eq.~\eqref{eq:rect_sum},
bringing the per-scale cost from $\Theta(NL^{2})$ to
$\Theta(N)$~\cite{Crow1984}. This optimisation is more impactful
for GB than for BC because GB evaluates $\Theta(N)$ overlapping
positions per scale rather than $\Theta(N/L^{2})$ disjoint cells,
making the naive cost grow as $L^{2}$ while the optimised cost
remains constant.

\subsection{Differential Box-Counting Method}
\label{sec:theory:dbc}

The Differential Box-Counting~(DBC) method, proposed by Sarkar
and Chaudhuri~\cite{Sarkar1994}, extends BC to grayscale images
by treating the intensity surface as a three-dimensional relief.
Let $I$ be a grayscale image of $N = W \times H$ pixels with
intensities in $\{0, \ldots, G{-}1\}$. For a given scale
$\varepsilon$, the image plane is partitioned into
$\lceil W/\varepsilon \rceil \times \lceil H/\varepsilon \rceil$
non-overlapping cells as in BC, but each cell is now extended
vertically: the intensity axis is divided into bands of height
$\delta = \lceil G / \lceil W/\varepsilon \rceil \rceil$, and the
number of boxes required to cover the local intensity range within
cell $(i, j)$ is

\begin{equation}
  n_{ij}(\varepsilon)
    = \left\lceil \frac{I_{\max}^{ij} - I_{\min}^{ij}}{\delta}
      \right\rceil + 1,
  \label{eq:dbc_count}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $I_{\max}^{ij}$ and $I_{\min}^{ij}$ are the
maximum and minimum intensities within the cell. The total count
$N(\varepsilon) = \sum_{i,j} n_{ij}(\varepsilon)$ is substituted
into Eq.~\eqref{eq:loglog} to estimate $D_B$ via regression over
$\mathcal{S}$.

By retaining gradient information through the intensity range
$I_{\max}^{ij} - I_{\min}^{ij}$, DBC avoids the threshold
sensitivity inherent to BC, at the cost of processing a richer
input representation. The naive formulation scans all
$\varepsilon^{2}$ pixels per cell to compute the local extrema,
yielding a per-scale cost of $\Theta(N)$ with a constant
proportional to $\varepsilon^{2}$. The optimised formulation
replaces this scan with two separate \textit{2D range-minimum and
range-maximum structures} built in $\Theta(N \log N)$ time and
$\Theta(N \log N)$ space via sparse tables, after which each
$(I_{\max}^{ij},\, I_{\min}^{ij})$ query is answered in
$\mathcal{O}(1)$, reducing the per-scale traversal to
$\Theta(N/\varepsilon^{2})$ with a unit constant — the same
asymptotic profile as the optimised BC applied to a grayscale
input.

\subsection{Blanket Method}
\label{sec:theory:bm}

The Blanket Method~(BM), introduced by Peleg et
al.~\cite{Peleg1984}, estimates the fractal dimension of the
intensity surface by measuring how its apparent area grows as a
morphological cover is progressively expanded around it at each
scale. Let $u_\varepsilon(x, y)$ and $b_\varepsilon(x, y)$ denote
the upper and lower \textit{blankets} at dilation level
$\varepsilon$, defined by the recurrences

\begin{align}
  u_\varepsilon(x,y) &= \max\!\Bigl(u_{\varepsilon-1}(x,y)+1,\;
    \max_{(s,t)\in\mathcal{N}} u_{\varepsilon-1}(s,t)\Bigr),
  \label{eq:blanket_u} \\
  b_\varepsilon(x,y) &= \min\!\Bigl(b_{\varepsilon-1}(x,y)-1,\;
    \min_{(s,t)\in\mathcal{N}} b_{\varepsilon-1}(s,t)\Bigr),
  \label{eq:blanket_b}
\end{align}

\noindent with $u_0 = b_0 = I$ and $\mathcal{N}$ denoting the
4-connected neighbourhood of $(x, y)$. The \textit{blanket
volume} at scale $\varepsilon$ is

\begin{equation}
  V(\varepsilon)
    = \sum_{x,y} \bigl[u_\varepsilon(x,y) - b_\varepsilon(x,y)\bigr],
  \label{eq:blanket_vol}
\end{equation}

\noindent and the scale-dependent area measure is obtained as
$A(\varepsilon) = \bigl[V(\varepsilon) - V(\varepsilon{-}1)\bigr]
/ 2$. Substituting $A(\varepsilon)$ for $M(\varepsilon)$ in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:loglog} and fitting the slope over $\mathcal{S}$
yields the fractal dimension estimate.

The naive formulation evaluates each recurrence step by scanning
the $K$-neighbourhood of every pixel, incurring $\Theta(N K^{2})$
per dilation level and $\Theta(N K^{2} \varepsilon_{\max})$ in
total. The optimised formulation decomposes the 2D neighbourhood
maximum and minimum into two successive 1D sliding-window
operations — one horizontal, one vertical — each solved in
$\Theta(N)$ using a \textit{monotonic deque} (double-ended queue)
per row or column~\cite{Lemire2006}. This separability reduces
the per-level cost to $\Theta(N)$ regardless of neighbourhood
size, and the total cost to $\Theta(N \varepsilon_{\max})$.
Because BM operates directly on the intensity surface without
binarisation, it retains the full gradient information of the
image, making it the most information-preserving of the four
methods at the price of higher computational demand.
